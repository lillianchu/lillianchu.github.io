Personal Safety Device


2021-2022 Capstone Project

return to engineering

Objective: Take a white space and use the design process to create an optimized product that solves the problem.


Final Product: Our final product was a personal safety device used as the "in-between" of feeling a bit unsafe, and calling the police. It uses a passive response mechanism that actives when pressed/clamped, and upon release sends your GPS location to a trusted contact.

  • Works without cell phone
  • Slim and discreet design that looks like headphone case or battery pack
  • Prevents crying wolf syndrome in the use of emergency phone call boxes

Skills: computer-aided design (CAD), product development, design for assembly, design for manufacturing, user testing, product benchmarking, rapid prototyping




White Space: Our team started with a complete white space. We performed problem-finding methods and through our decisions matrix, we narrowed our options to general personal safety.

We polled college students to see where they felt the least safe, and there was an overwhelming response for walking alone at night.



Problem Statement: How might we create safer experiences and increase situational awareness for students walking home late at night after a party/social event while potentially inebriated or fatigued?


Initial User Research and Stakeholder Interviews:

After narrowing the situation down to walking home from an off-campus party, we journey mapped the scenario and began stakeholder interviews.

  • Campus Safety (8)

  • Student Safety (10)

  • Self-Defense Experts (3)

    Key Takeaways:

  • Students lacking situational awareness
  • No use of defensive weapons unless properly trained
  • Lack of use of emergency phone boxes
  • Stigma with using emergency phone box blue light system
  • Fear of being ignored or mishandled by university police
  • Little to no communication from universities surrounding available safety mechanisms

Need-Finding Research:

We analyzed and benchmarked various products on the market to find where the white spaces and areas of improvement were. We saw that there were a lot of products for defense in an altercation, but not much for before or after a physical altercation.

Brainstorming: We began our ideation phase through brainstorming several ideas and sorting them into different categories and sub-categories based on whether or not it would be on your person or something implemented in the environment.

We then performed card-sorting exercises with users, and took the top 4 ranked ideas and turned them into low-fidelity mockups.

Personal Blue Light Switch: activates emergency call box once button is pressed

Backpack Camera: allows user to see behind them

Street Lights: motion-sensored lights allow you to see traces of people walking

Chest Strap Light: illuminates path handsfree

Final Design Direction:

Our team decided to go in the direction of a personal safety switch, a device that when held, activates a system that will send a text message if you release the device. This device is simple and limits error if fatigued or intoxicated, customizable in the scope of who it alerts (trusted contact rather than police), and it is relevant to the biggest concerns of walking home alone and in that the form factor could feasibly be brought to social events.

Higher-Fidelity Prototypes:

We then decided to work on higher-fidelity prototypes. Our process was:

  • Initial ideation and sketching
  • Initial mockups from existing grips
  • Ergonomics Research
    • User Testing
    • Ergonomic grip testing (20 users)
    • Forms of actuation testing (40 users)
    • Tension testing (15 users)
    • Lock testing (15 users)
  • Development of needs and requirements

Looks-Like Prototypes: With our three actuation options, we took user feedback and made 3D models and optimized them based on new requirement specifications. Models were created and printed at the maximum size which we used for another round of testing.

Synthesizing Feedback:

We performed another round of user testing focusing on ergonomic feel and visual perception of users. The new design combined the curved handle of the trigger with the hinge mechanism that users felt was more substantial and satisfying. Finally, we added a central locking mechanism actuated by the thumb.

Discussions with Campus Security: Prior to performing user testing with our final design, we reached out to the Director of Environmental Health and Safety, Chris Yohe, and NUPD Deputy Chief Eric Chin to approve our testing plan with security personnel. Their response was:


“Although the device is harmless, it does resemble a folding pocket knife, which might raise some concerns.”


“Not ideal for an event where security checks are in place.”


“Even though this item is inert, the appearance of the item, seen by a third party or even recovery if lost as you cited, may cause alarm, concern and disruption.”


Design Pivot:

Based on this feedback, our team made a design pivot to the linear trigger mockup. Although not ideal ergonomically based on our previous user testing, the linear trigger design offered a number of advantages.

From previous user testing:

  • Inconspicuous/unassuming
  • Lopoks like phone charger or headphones case
  • Easily pocketable

Design for Manufacturing Advantages:

  • Less parts
  • Simple geometry
  • More space-efficient in terms of internal electronics packaging

Design for Assembly Advantages:

  • Less parts
  • Fewer and less complex assembly steps

After reaching out to campus security again, Chris Yohe stated, “I think this design is a step in the right direction. It reminds me of a grip-strengthener device.”


From top to bottom: AirPods case, plastic 3D print, resin 3D print

Optimization: We created maximum and minimum-sized prototypes for our design based on the specs from initial user testing.


Locking Mechanism: easily accessible by thumb

  • Lock tabs that protect internals
  • Sliding lock with textured top
  • Hooked trigger shaft stopped by catch
  • Hinging catch:
    • Separate piece for print
    • Living hinge for injection-molded part
  • Lock position detents

Provisional Patent: contact for further information


Design for Assembly:

  • Simple symmetric alignment for springs
  • Countersunk screw holes with tamper-proof bitting
  • Internal lip of cover aids with alignment and press fit
  • Keyway pass through for hooked trigger shaft that allows for insertion when rotated

Design for Manufacturing:

  • No overhangs in any parts
  • Increased access to internal cavity
  • Uniform wall thickness
  • No sharp external corners
  • Bosses and ribs to support columns and holes
